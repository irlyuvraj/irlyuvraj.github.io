// engineering_highlights
Engineering Highlights
Unified multimodal perception

One model (Qwen3-Omni, via vLLM) handles video and audio jointly, replacing what would otherwise be separate VAD, ASR, and vision-encoder components.

Diagnosing a production inference bug

Traced a ~50% output parse-failure rate to a known vLLM/Qwen3 compatibility issue and validated a fix across the full deployment.

Model-behavior-driven design

The model is unreliable at structured fields but reliable at focused yes/no questions — this shaped both the visual grounding gate and the completion verifier.

// known_limitations
Known Limitations

Open-vocabulary understanding, but closed-vocabulary execution — the robot only performs its trained skills, and adding one requires new demonstration data.

The model is reliable at focused yes/no visual checks but weak at fine-grained grounding (e.g., color naming), which the perception design works around rather than solves.

The anticipatory speech path trades selectivity for speed: it can fragment compound commands and needs confirmation gates that add back some latency.

About

I'm a graduate researcher at The University of Osaka's Ishiguro Laboratory, on a university-recommended MEXT scholarship, graduating September 2026. My research focuses on multimodal AI systems that can supervise and intervene in real-time robot behavior — predicting human intent before it's explicit, and deciding when a robot should keep going versus stop.

Before graduate school, I spent 3 years as a software engineer at Accenture, working on large-scale enterprise systems, data pipelines, and quality engineering. That background shapes how I approach research: I care as much about a system being reliable and reproducible as I do about it being novel.

Outside of research, I'm continuing to study Japanese, and I occasionally run informal STEM workshops for children.

Why this approach: most interruption systems either require continuous teleoperation or hard-coded voice commands. Here, the robot acts autonomously by default, and a general-purpose model — used entirely through prompting, no fine-tuning on the perception side — supervises it well enough to intervene at the right moment.
